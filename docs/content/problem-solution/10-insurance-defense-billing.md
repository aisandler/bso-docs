## What Are Common Insurance Carrier Billing Requirements?

Insurance carrier billing requirements vary by carrier, but common themes include:

### Task-Based Billing Codes (UTBMS Codes)

Most carriers require Uniform Task-Based Management System (UTBMS) codes categorizing each time entry by legal task performed. The system includes:

- **L100 series:** Case assessment, development of case strategy
- **L200 series:** Pre-trial pleadings and motions
- **L300 series:** Discovery
- **L400 series:** Trial preparation and trial
- **L500 series:** Alternative dispute resolution
- **L600 series:** Appeal

Each code has subcategories providing granular detail. For example, L310 (Interrogatories) splits into L311 (Propound), L312 (Defend), and L313 (Review answers). Billing staff must assign appropriate codes to every time entry, requiring detailed understanding of legal work performed.

The challenge intensifies when carriers customize UTBMS codes with firm-specific interpretations. What one carrier categorizes as L220 (Drafting/responding to motions) another might require as L230 (Written discovery), depending on motion type and context.

### Phase-Based Budget Caps

Insurance carriers establish budgets for different litigation phases: pleadings, discovery, motion practice, trial preparation, trial. Firms must track time and expenses against phase budgets, requesting approval before exceeding caps.

This requires sophisticated matter management:

- **Real-time budget tracking:** Attorneys need current visibility into budget utilization
- **Proactive communication:** Firms must request budget increases before exhausting allocations
- **Work prioritization:** Attorneys must make strategic decisions about task prioritization within budget constraints
- **Variance justification:** Budget overruns require detailed explanations of necessity

Firms lacking robust budget tracking systems discover overruns at month-end billing, creating collection challenges and carrier relationship strain.

### Block Billing Prohibitions

Most carriers prohibit "block billing"—time entries describing multiple tasks without time allocation to each component. This rejected entry: "Draft motion to dismiss; research case law; telephone conference with client (2.5 hours)" violates carrier guidelines requiring separate entries for each task.

Compliant time entry requires:

- **Task-specific entries:** Each distinct task gets separate time entry
- **Descriptive detail:** Entries must explain what was done, not just category labels
- **Reasonable time allocations:** Time must align with task complexity

Attorneys accustomed to block billing find this tedious. Billing staff must educate attorneys on compliant entry practices and edit non-compliant entries before invoice submission—a time-consuming process requiring legal knowledge to accurately split block entries.

### Detailed Expense Documentation

Insurance carriers scrutinize expenses with detailed approval requirements:

- **Expert witness fees:** Pre-approval required, with justification of necessity and rate reasonableness
- **Travel expenses:** Advance approval for out-of-town travel, mileage documentation, per diem limitations
- **Court costs:** Contemporaneous documentation of filing fees, deposition costs, court reporter charges
- **Technology expenses:** Justification of research costs, litigation support expenses, e-discovery tools

Each expense type requires specific documentation standards. Missing documentation results in automatic expense rejection, even when legitimately incurred.

### Rate Compliance and Attorney Staffing

Carriers negotiate discounted hourly rates with panel law firms, often with different rates by attorney experience level: partner, senior associate, junior associate, paralegal. Firms must track time against approved rate structures and staff matters appropriately.

Carrier guidelines often include "reasonable attorney staffing" requirements:

- **Single attorney attendance:** Conference calls and hearings generally require only one attorney
- **Experience-appropriate staffing:** Routine tasks should be delegated to junior attorneys and paralegals
- **Detailed collaboration justification:** Multiple attorneys working on the same task requires explanation

Violations result in write-downs. If two partners attend a routine hearing billed at $400/hour each, the carrier might approve payment for one attorney at senior associate rate ($250/hour), writing down $550 from submitted billing.

## How Do Billing Guidelines Differ Across Major Insurance Carriers?

Each major insurance carrier maintains distinct billing guidelines, creating complexity for firms working with multiple carriers:

### Sample Carrier Variations

**Carrier A Requirements:**
- UTBMS codes mandatory with carrier-specific customizations
- Phase budgets strictly enforced with automatic invoice holds for overruns
- Block billing automatic rejection with entire invoice return
- 0.1 hour minimum billing increment
- Travel time billed at 50% of attorney standard rate

**Carrier B Requirements:**
- UTBMS codes optional but encouraged
- Overall matter budgets without phase subdivision
- Block billing permitted for tasks totaling less than 0.5 hours
- 0.2 hour minimum billing increment
- Travel time billed at full rate with narrative justification

**Carrier C Requirements:**
- Carrier-proprietary task codes replacing UTBMS
- No formal budgets but "reasonableness" review of all billing
- Block billing prohibited without exception
- 0.1 hour minimum billing increment
- Travel time billed at 75% of attorney standard rate for distances exceeding 50 miles

These variations require billing staff to maintain detailed knowledge of each carrier's specific requirements. Invoice preparation for Carrier A looks completely different from Carrier B, even when billing for identical legal work.

## How Can Mid-Size Insurance Defense Firms Improve Billing Efficiency?

Effective insurance defense billing requires specialized expertise and systematic processes:

### 1. Invest in Specialized Billing Staff Training

Insurance defense billing demands knowledge that general legal billing experience does not provide. Effective training includes:

**Carrier Guideline Education:** Deep learning of specific carrier requirements, updated quarterly as guidelines change.

**UTBMS Code Mastery:** Understanding task code definitions and appropriate application to diverse legal activities.

**Electronic Billing Platform Proficiency:** Technical training on submission platforms, error resolution, and resubmission procedures.

**Attorney Communication Skills:** Diplomatic approaches to educating attorneys about guideline compliance and editing non-compliant time entries.

This specialized training requires significant time investment—typically 3-6 months before new billing staff achieve independent competency in insurance defense billing. The training burden contributes to billing staff turnover challenges when firms lack systematic training programs.

### 2. Implement Proactive Compliance Systems

Reactive approaches to guideline compliance—discovering violations at month-end billing—create crisis management and high write-down rates. Proactive systems prevent non-compliance through:

**Daily Time Entry Review:** Billing staff review attorney time entries daily, identifying and correcting non-compliant entries immediately rather than accumulating problems until month-end.

**Automated Compliance Validation:** Configure practice management software to reject entries violating known guidelines: missing UTBMS codes, block billing patterns, budget overruns.

**Attorney Training:** Regular training sessions educating attorneys about carrier requirements and common compliance failures.

**Guideline Update Communications:** Systematic communication of carrier guideline changes to affected attorneys, with specific examples of required changes.

These proactive approaches require more billing staff time for daily review but dramatically reduce month-end crisis management and write-down rates.
